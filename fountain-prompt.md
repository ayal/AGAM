# Fire & Water Fountain — 3D Animation Prompt

A technical, technology-agnostic description for recreating Yaacov Agam's
"Fire & Water" fountain as a generative 3D animation.

---

**Structure.** Five stacked cylindrical rings form a bulging vertical tower — small at the base, swelling to a tall, wide ring in the middle, then tapering again toward the top (radii roughly 7 / 12 / 16 / 10.5 / 6.5 in arbitrary units, with the middle ring also the tallest). The whole tower stands about four times taller than its widest ring. Each ring is not a smooth cylinder but a **zigzag accordion** of flat vertical panels pleated in and out around the circumference, giving every tier a cog-like, toothed silhouette, so a different painted composition resolves depending on which side you view it from. The pleated top and bottom caps that close each ring are tinted to match the colors of the panels beneath them.

**Panel compositions.** Each pleated panel is a vertical strip divided into **3–5 stacked regions of varying width**, each region filled with exactly one motif drawn as flat, pixel-like cells with hard edges and no anti-aliasing: vertical stripes, horizontal stripes, a checkerboard, concentric **square or diamond rings** radiating from the center, a simple two-tone split (top/bottom or left/right halves), or a centered **rectangle/frame** (solid, hollow-outlined, or with a contrasting center dot). Stripes are 1–2 cells wide, checkers 1–3. At most **one** region per panel may instead be a single large, **perfectly round circle** — the only true curve in the whole piece — a vivid solid disc sitting on a lighter tonal checkerboard ground. Different panels carry different region layouts, which is exactly what makes a new composition resolve as the viewing angle changes.

**Palette.** Pick **one** cohesive color scheme for the entire piece and stay within it — a small, discrete, high-chroma set (e.g. red, yellow, green, blue, purple) rather than a full rainbow, always anchored by a near-black and an off-white. Within any single panel, draw only from an **analogous run** — 2–4 hues that sit next to each other on the color wheel (e.g. yellow→green→blue), never harsh equal-area clashes like red directly beside yellow. Keep hues bold and saturated; get variety from a lighter tint or darker shade of the *same* hue, with an occasional neutral gray as a companion break rather than a second strong color. Panel grounds are a warm off-white. About a quarter of renders are done entirely in pure black-and-white (no grays); in that case the background shifts to neutral gray so neither black nor white disappears.

**Water and fire.** At the base sits a still, circular **pool** that reads as a water surface: deep-to-light blue, animated with soft summed-sine ripples, a faint shimmer, a mild bright rim (Fresnel), and a live mirror reflection of the fountain above it. Blue **water jets** — streams of particles arcing and falling under gravity — spray outward from the top three rings; their pressure visibly builds and releases on a slow ramp and occasionally surges much higher than normal, plus a single tall, near-vertical center jet that pulses upward, holds, and then rests. Crowning the very top is the **fire** of "Fire & Water": a flame of rising, spreading particles with flickering height, colored hot yellow fading through orange to smoke-gray as each particle ages.

**Render style.** Keep it flat and gallery-like, not photoreal. The painted panels and caps are matte and self-lit — no cast shadows, no specular highlights, no glossy plastic look; the colors should stay pure and poster-bright. The *only* reflective, shiny element in the scene is the pool. The background is a flat, neutral warm-gray with no gradient or environment, so both the colors and the silhouette stay legible.

**Motion and camera.** Everything moves at a calm, meditative tempo, not an energetic one. The individual rings rotate slowly and independently, alternating spin direction from tier to tier, while the entire tower also turns gently about its vertical axis — a fraction of a degree per frame. The water-jet pressure swells and falls over several seconds; the center jet pulses on a longer cycle; the fire flickers quickly on top of a slow rise and fall. The camera sits pulled back and low, nearly front-on, framing the full tower centered with headroom above for the fire and the occasional high jet surge, and a little room below for the pool. Critically, let the camera **orbit slowly** around the fountain rather than holding still — as the angle changes, the pleated panels shift and different hidden compositions resolve on each ring, which is the entire point of this kinetic, agamograph-style design.
